According to the general requirements of the Canadian Electrical Code (CEC), the total continuous load allowed in a 15 A15\text{ A}, 120 V120\text{ V} circuit is 1440 W1440\text{ W}.

This is derived from the requirement that a circuit's continuous load (a load expected to operate for a total of more than one hour in any two-hour period) must not exceed 80%80\% of the rating of the overcurrent device (the 15 A15\text{ A} circuit breaker).

⚡️ Calculation

The calculation is based on the application of the 80%80\% rule for standard circuit breakers that are not specifically marked for 100%100\% continuous operation (which is the typical scenario in residential and most commercial installations).

1. Total Circuit Capacity (Volt-Amperes)

The maximum total capacity of the circuit is calculated using the formula P=E×IP = E \times I (Power = Voltage ×\times Current), where the result is expressed in Volt-Amperes (VA\text{VA}), which is equivalent to Watts (W\text{W}) for purely resistive loads:

Total Capacity=120 V×15 A=1800 VA(or 1800 W)\text{Total Capacity} = 120\text{ V} \times 15\text{ A} = 1800\text{ VA} (\text{or } 1800\text{ W})

2. Maximum Allowed Load (80% Rule)

The CEC (Rule 8-104(6)) limits the continuous load to 80%80\% of the circuit rating for standard circuit breakers:

Maximum Allowed Load=Total Capacity×0.80\text{Maximum Allowed Load} = \text{Total Capacity} \times 0.80

Maximum Allowed Load=1800 W×0.80=1440 W\text{Maximum Allowed Load} = 1800\text{ W} \times 0.80 = 1440\text{ W}

This 1440 W1440\text{ W} value is the maximum limit for any load that will be running continuously. In practice, it's generally used as the design limit for the total connected load (including both continuous and non-continuous devices) on the circuit to maintain a safe operating margin.

The 80%80\% rule ensures that the circuit components, especially the breaker in a panelboard, do not overheat when carrying a heavy load for an extended period.
