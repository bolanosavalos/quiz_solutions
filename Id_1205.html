The Two-Stage Calculation

In the CEC, there are two distinct limits for overcurrent protection: the Maximum Rating (Table 29) and the Absolute Maximum Exception (Rule 28-200 Subrule 4).

  1. Standard Maximum (Table 29): For a 20 hp, 575 V motor, the FLC from Table 44 is 22 A. Table 29 allows 175% for a time-delay fuse. 22 A×1.75=38.5 A22 \text{ A} \times 1.75 = \mathbf{38.5 \text{ A}} According to Rule 28-200(3), you cannot exceed this value. Therefore, you must "round down" to the nearest standard fuse, which is 35 A.

  2. The "Exception" (Rule 28-200 Subrule 4): You only use the larger multiplier (up to 225% for time-delay fuses) if the overcurrent device selected in the first step "will not permit the motor to start." 22 A×2.25=49.5 A22 \text{ A} \times 2.25 = \mathbf{49.5 \text{ A}} If the 35 A fuse blows during startup, the code then permits you to go up to a maximum of 45 A (the standard size not exceeding 49.5 A).

Why 35 A is the "Correct" Initial Answer

In many certification exams (like the Red Seal), if the question asks for the "largest" fuse permitted without explicitly stating that the motor "fails to start" on a standard fuse, they are looking for the Table 29 result.

  • 175% Limit: 38.5 A → 35 A is the largest standard size that does not exceed the limit.
  • 225% Limit: 49.5 A → 45 A is the absolute ceiling if the 35 A fails.

If your specific course or exam context implies the standard application of Rule 28-200 (without the "failure to start" condition), then 35 A is indeed the correct technical choice.
