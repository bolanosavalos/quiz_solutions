1. The Conductor Count (10 Units)

If there are 5 wires coming in and 5 wires leaving, and we are not adding a separate "bonus" volume for a bond (because the raceway provides the bond):

  • Total Conductor Count: 10 units.

2. The Wire Connector Count (2 Units)

Per Rule 12-3034 2) g), we deduct one conductor volume for every two wire connectors.

  • For 5 wire nuts, we have 2 full pairs.
  • Total Connector Count: 2 units.

3. Total Unit Sum

  • 10 (Wires) + 2 (Connectors) = 12 units.

4. The Final Volume Calculation

  • The 125% Rule for Motor Conductors

Per Rule 28-106 1), the insulated conductors supplying a single motor must have an ampacity of at least 125% of the motor's full load current rating if the motor is rated for continuous duty.

The Calculation:

20A (FLA) x 1.25 = 25A

2. Selecting the Wire Size (Table 2)

We look at Table 2 (Allowable Ampacities for Copper Conductors) under the 75°C column (the standard temperature rating for most modern equipment terminations): 

Wire Size (AWG)
 75°C Ampacity
No. 12 AWG    25 A

Using the 28.7 mL value for No. 12 AWG: 12×28.7 mL=344.4 mL12 \times 28.7 \text{ mL} = \mathbf{344.4 \text{ mL}}

Why the 4-inch Square Box Fails

This is the "gotcha" moment for students. When we look at Table 23 for standard box capacities:

  • 1 1/2 in. × 4 in. Square Box: 344 mL.

Since 344.4 mL is greater than 344 mL, the 4-inch square box is legally too small. Even though it is only over by 0.4 mL, the Code does not allow for "rounding down" in this situation.

Resulting Box Selection

To be code-compliant, you must move to the next size up in the table:

  • Minimum Standard Box: 1 1/2 in. × 4 11/16 in. Square Box (Capacity: 475 mL).

Summary for your Students

The textbook is likely using this specific example to show that wire connectors must be included in the count. By including those 2 units for the connectors, the total volume hits 344.4 mL, which "kills" the 4-inch square box and forces the use of the 4 11/16 in. box.
