Between X and Y, the minimum required is:

4 conductors

Here’s the correct breakdown:

  1. Black - Constant hot

    • Marretted to the receptacle hot.
    • Also feeds the common of the 3-way (S3).

  2. Red - Traveller #1

  3. Blue - Traveller #2

  4. White - Neutral

    • Needed to keep the receptacle energized at all times.

So the cable between X and Y must carry:

  • 1 constant hot
  • 2 travellers
  • 1 neutral

That makes 4 insulated conductors minimum.



Between the two light outlet boxes, you need to carry: 14/5 between the lights.

  1. Neutral (white) - shared neutral for both lights
  2. Constant hot (black) - feed-through
  3. Traveller 1 (red)
  4. Traveller 2 (blue)
  5. Switched hot (usually another color, e.g., yellow) - returning from the 3-way to the light

That’s 5 current-carrying conductors, so a 14/5 is appropriate.

In a 3-way system where:

  • Feed is at one location
  • Switching is at another
  • Lights are between boxes

14/4 from the left 3-way (S3) to the left light is perfectly acceptable in that layout 👍

Here’s why it makes sense:

Between the left S3 and the left light, you typically need:

  1. Neutral (white) – required in the switch box under modern code

  2. Constant hot (black) – feed into the common of the 3-way in the example is orange.

  3. Traveller #1 (red)

  4. Traveller #2 (blue)

That’s 4 insulated conductors, so 14/4 is correct.

Why not 14/3?

You’d be short one conductor if:

  • You must carry neutral into the switch box (current code requirement), and

  • You need two travellers plus a feed.

Without the neutral, older installs sometimes used 14/3 — but that no longer satisfies code where a neutral is required in the switch box.

Trade Summary

Left S3 → Left Light
✔ Neutral
✔ Constant hot
✔ Traveller 1
✔ Traveller 2

= 14/4 minimum
