The volts per turn on the secondary winding of an ideal transformer are equal to the volts per turn on the primary winding.

Since the volts per turn on the primary winding are given as 6 Volts6\text{ Volts}, the volts per turn on the secondary winding are also 6 Volts6\text{ Volts}.

Explanation

The volts per turn (VN\frac{V}{N}) is a fundamental characteristic of a transformer's magnetic circuit and is the same for both the primary and secondary windings in an ideal transformer.

This is because both windings share the same magnetic flux (Φ\Phi). According to Faraday's Law of Induction, the voltage induced in a coil is proportional to the number of turns (NN) and the rate of change of the magnetic flux (dΦdt\frac{d\Phi}{dt}):

V=N(dΦdt)V = N \left(\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\right)

Rearranging for the volts per turn:

VN=dΦdt\frac{V}{N} = \frac{d\Phi}{dt}

Since the rate of change of flux (dΦdt\frac{d\Phi}{dt}) is the same for both the primary (pp) and secondary (ss) windings, the volts per turn must also be the same:

(VN)p=(VN)s\left(\frac{V}{N}\right)_p = \left(\frac{V}{N}\right)_s

The turn ratio (10:110:1) of the transformer determines the total voltage and number of turns, but it does not change the volts per turn ratio.
