In the 2024 Canadian Electrical Code (CEC), the notes for Table D3 provide specific "correction factors" that adjust the base resistance (KK) value of the conductor.

2. Required Resistance per km (Kmax)

The DC voltage drop formula is used for this battery-supplied system. The formula is rearranged to find the maximum resistance per kilometer (Kmax) required to keep the VD at or below 0.3 V.

Kmax=VDmax×1000f×I×L

  • VDmax=0.3 V
  • f=2 (System factor for a two-wire DC circuit, representing the total length of the outgoing and return conductors)
  • I=4 A (Current)
  • L=15 m (One-way length)

Kmax=0.3×10002×4×15 = 300 120 = 2.5 ohms/km

The conductor must have a K value (resistance per km) of 2.5 ohms/km or less for the voltage drop to be compliant.


For your 6V emergency lighting system, you are absolutely correct that these notes allow for a significant derating of the resistance, which makes No. 8 AWG the correct minimum size.

⚡ Breaking Down Notes 3 and 4 (Table D3)

When you calculate your maximum allowable resistance (Kmax=2.5 Ω/kmK_{max} = 2.5 \text{ } \Omega/\text{km}), the CEC allows you to apply these multipliers to the "Copper DC" column values if your circuit meets certain conditions.

Note 3: The Extra-Low Voltage Factor (0.86)

This is the "big" derate for low-voltage systems. It states that for extra-low-voltage power circuits where the load current is not more than two-thirds of the conductor's ampacity (based on the 60°C column of Table 2), you can multiply the KK value by 0.86.

  • For No. 8 AWG: Table 2 (60°C) is 40A. Your 4A load is way less than 26.6A (two-thirds).
  • Result: You apply the 0.86 multiplier.

Note 4: The Temperature Adjustment (0.95)

This note is used if the equipment/conductors are operating at 60°C instead of the standard 75°C used for the table's baseline.

  • Emergency lighting battery packs and remote lamps are almost always rated for 60°C terminations.
  • Result: You apply the 0.95 multiplier.
