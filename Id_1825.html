To understand this calculation from an engineering and trade perspective, we have to look at how the Canadian Electrical Code (CEC) balances the physical capacity of a transformer against the actual protection provided by a fuse.

1. The General Rule: Sizing for Full Capacity

Under normal conditions, a transformer is sized to handle its full rated power (25 kVA25\text{ kVA}) plus a safety margin for continuous operation and inrush current.

The Calculation:

  • Secondary Full Load Current (IsI_s): Is=25,000 VA240 V=104.17 AI_s = \frac{25,000\text{ VA}}{240\text{ V}} = 104.17\text{ A}

  • The 125% Requirement (Rule 26-258): The code generally requires conductors to be sized at 125%125\% of the rated current to prevent overheating during continuous use. 104.17 A×1.25=130.21 A104.17\text{ A} \times 1.25 = 130.21\text{ A}

  • The Result: Under the general rule, you would look at Table 2 (75°C) and select No. 1 AWG (rated for 135A).

2. The "Choke" Concept: Using a Smaller Primary Fuse

In your specific scenario, the primary fuse is 45A. Let's look at the primary side:

  • Primary Full Load Current (IpI_p): 25,000 VA/600V=41.67 A25,000\text{ VA} / 600\text{V} = 41.67\text{ A}.
  • Maximum Allowed Fuse: 41.67 A×1.25=52.08 A41.67\text{ A} \times 1.25 = 52.08\text{ A} (Standard fuse would be 50A or 60A).

By choosing a 45A fuse, you have "choked" the transformer. The fuse will blow long before the transformer ever reaches its maximum 25 kVA25\text{ kVA} output. Because the primary fuse limits the total energy entering the system, the secondary conductors are physically protected by that same primary fuse.

3. The Engineering Exemption: Rule 26-256 3)

This is where the engineering standard and the code meet. Rule 26-256 3) allows you to size conductors based on the demand load (what the system is actually allowed to draw) rather than the transformer’s theoretical maximum, provided the wires are protected.

The "Ratio Method" Calculation: To find out when that 45A primary fuse will trip relative to the secondary side, we use the transformer's voltage ratio:

  • Voltage Ratio: 600V/240V=2.5600\text{V} / 240\text{V} = \mathbf{2.5}
  • Max Secondary Current (Is−maxI_{s-max}): 45A (Primary Fuse)×2.5 (Ratio)=112.5A45\text{A (Primary Fuse)} \times 2.5\text{ (Ratio)} = 112.5\text{A}

Why this is Safe:

Physically, it is impossible for 113A113\text{A} to flow on the secondary side without the 45A45\text{A} fuse on the primary side blowing. Therefore, the secondary conductor only needs to be rated to handle what the fuse allows: 112.5A.

4. Final Selection (Table 2)

Now we take our "Choked" demand load of 112.5A to Table 2 using the 75°C column (for TW75):

  • No. 3 AWG: Rated for 100A (Too small; the fuse would allow 112.5A, which could melt this wire).
  • No. 2 AWG: Rated for 115A.

Final Answer: Because 115A > 112.5A, the No. 2 AWG conductor is the minimum size that is legally and physically protected by the 45A primary fuse.

Summary: The general rule (125%) says use No. 1 AWG. The engineering exemption (Rule 26-256 3) recognizes the 45A fuse "chokes" the system, allowing you to safely drop down to No. 2 AWG.
