The mention of "remote lamps" often refers to emergency lighting systems governed by CEC Section 46 (Emergency Systems, Unit Equipment, and Auxiliary Systems).

If the remote lamps in your question are part of an Emergency Lighting System (like unit equipment remote heads) governed by Rule 46-210 or 46-306, then the voltage drop allowance is 5%, as stated in the notes for that section of the CEC.

This change is critical because it directly increases the maximum allowable voltage drop, which in turn increases the permitted current.

Recalculation based on CEC Section 46 (Emergency Lighting)

1. Determine Maximum Permissible Voltage Drop (VDmax\text{VD}_{max})

Assuming the system is for emergency lighting (remote lamps) where the voltage drop limit is 5% of the supply voltage:

VDmax=6 V×0.05=0.30 V\text{VD}_{max} = 6 \text{ V} \times 0.05 = 0.30 \text{ V}

2. Identify the Voltage Drop Formula and Required Constant

We use the same rearranged CEC Table D3 formula for a 2-wire DC circuit (where the system factor f=2f=2):

I=VD×1000K×f×LI = \frac{\text{VD} \times 1000}{K \times f \times L}

Constants used:

  • VD=0.30 V\text{VD} = 0.30 \text{ V} (new 5% value)
  • K=2.54 ohm/kmK = 2.54 \text{ ohm}/\text{km} (Assumed value for #8 AWG Cu\text{Cu}, 75∘C75^\circ\text{C} from Table D3)
  • f=2f = 2
  • L=10.2 mL = 10.2 \text{ m}

3. Solve for Maximum Current (II)

I=0.30 V×10002.54ohmkm×2×10.2 mI = \frac{0.30 \text{ V} \times 1000}{2.54 \frac{\text{ohm}}{\text{km}} \times 2 \times 10.2 \text{ m}}

I=30051.816I = \frac{300}{51.816}

I≈5.79 AI \approx 5.79 \text{ A}

4. Conclusion with Note 4 Applied

If the remote lamps circuit falls under CEC Rule 46-210 or 46-306 for emergency lighting, the maximum current the circuit is permitted to supply is approximately 5.79 Amperes.
