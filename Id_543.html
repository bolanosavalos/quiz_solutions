While specific rules can vary slightly between editions and provincial amendments to the Canadian Electrical Code (CEC), the minimum requirement for branch circuits from the distribution panel serving a garage for a single dwelling unit is typically at least one 15 A or 20 A branch circuit.

The circuit must be a 125 V receptacle branch circuit dedicated to the garage, which can also supply one or more permanently installed lighting outlets, but may not be required to be dedicated solely to the garage receptacles.

However, a more common and often required practice, especially with modern appliance use and the prevalence of electric vehicle charging, is to install:

  • One dedicated 120 V, 20 A branch circuit for receptacle outlets. This circuit usually cannot supply any other outlets (like lights or a garage door opener), except for readily accessible outdoor receptacle outlets on the same circuit, depending on the specific CEC rule in force.
  • One or more additional branch circuits for lighting and other loads (like the garage door opener). These are often 15 A circuits.

Therefore, for practical and often code-compliant purposes, two or more circuits are often installed, with at least one being a dedicated 20 A circuit for receptacles.

The rules regarding branch circuits for dwelling units, including garages, can generally be found in Section 26 of the Canadian Electrical Code, Part I (the Safety Standard for Electrical Installations).

Specifically, you should look for the rules dealing with:

  • Rule 26-654 f) series: Branch circuits for dwelling units
  • Rule 26-726 c) series: Receptacle requirements for single dwelling units

For the most precise and up-to-date information, including amendments specific to your region, you must consult the latest edition of the CEC, Part I and any local electrical inspection authority bulletins.
