Appendix B note for Rule 14-012, which clarifies the "default" interrupting ratings used in the Canadian Electrical Code (CEC) when a device isn't specifically marked otherwise.


The Answer Explained

According to the text you provided from Appendix B of Rule 14-012, the Code establishes these baseline "assumed" ratings:

  • 5,000 A: For breakers rated 100 A or less and 250 V or less.
  • 10,000 A (10 kA): For breakers rated above 100 A or above 250 V.

Because your specific example is a 200 A, 208 V, 3-phase breaker, it falls into the second category (it is above 100 A). Therefore, unless the breaker is specifically marked with a higher value (like 22 kA or 65 kA), the CEC considers its minimum interrupting rating to be:

10,000 A (10 kA)

Why This Text Matters

This Appendix B note is effectively the "default rule" for designers and inspectors in Canada. Here is the breakdown of why it exists:

  • Marking Rule: Most modern breakers are marked with their actual rating (e.g., "10k" or "22k"). However, the Code needs a fallback. If you find an old or unmarked breaker that is 200 A, the Code "credits" it with a 10,000 A capacity.
  • Safety Threshold: If your calculated available fault current at the 200 A panel is 12,000 A, and you have a breaker that is not marked with an interrupting rating, you cannot use it. Why? Because the Code assumes an unmarked breaker of that size is only good for 10,000 A. You would be required to install a breaker explicitly marked as having a higher rating (like 22 kA).
  • Symmetrical vs. Asymmetrical: You noticed the word "symmetrical". This refers to the AC current wave. In Canada, we almost always perform our fault calculations in "RMS Symmetrical Amperes," which aligns perfectly with this Appendix B guidance.
