The Problem

Question: What is the voltage drop across Load A and Load B when the neutral becomes open, or "broken"?


The Solution

1. Find the Resistance of each load

First, we determine the resistance based on the original 120V120\text{V} design:

  • Load A: IA=14kW120V=116.7A      →      RA=120V116.7A≈1.0Ω

I_A = \frac{14\text{kW}}{120\text{V}} = 116.7\text{A} \rightarrow R_A = \frac{120\text{V}}{116.7\text{A}} \approx \mathbf{1.0\Omega}

  • Load B: IB=7kW120V=58.3A         →        RB=120V58.3A≈2.0ΩI_B = \frac{7\text{kW}}{120\text{V}} = 58.3\text{A} \rightarrow R_B = \frac{120\text{V}}{58.3\text{A}} \approx \mathbf{2.0\Omega}

2. Calculate the current with a Broken Neutral

With the neutral open, the loads are in series across the full 240V240\text{V} line. The total resistance is 1.0Ω+2.0Ω=3.0Ω1.0\Omega + 2.0\Omega = 3.0\Omega.

IBrokenN=240V3.0Ω=80AI_{Broken N} = \frac{240\text{V}}{3.0\Omega} = \mathbf{80\text{A}}

3. Calculate the resulting Voltage Drops (V=I×RV = I \times R)

  • Voltage at Load A: VA=80A×1.0Ω=80VV_A = 80\text{A} \times 1.0\Omega = \mathbf{80\text{V}}
  • Voltage at Load B: VB=80A×2.0Ω=160VV_B = 80\text{A} \times 2.0\Omega = \mathbf{160\text{V}}

Summary: Load A experiences an undervoltage of 80V80\text{V}, while Load B receives a dangerous overvoltage of 160V160\text{V}.
