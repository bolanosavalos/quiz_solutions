In the context of fire alarm systems, understanding how Normally Open (N/O) detectors are wired is crucial for both system functionality and "supervision" (the system's ability to detect a broken wire).

Why Diagram B is Correct

In a standard fire alarm initiating circuit (specifically a Conventional Zone), detectors are wired in parallel.

  • Normally Open (N/O): Under normal conditions, the switch inside the detector is open. No current flows through the detector itself.
  • The "Short": When a detector senses heat or smoke, the internal switch closes, creating a short circuit between the two supply lines.
  • The Alarm Trigger: The Fire Alarm Control Panel (FACP) senses this sudden increase in current (the "short") and triggers the alarm.

In Diagram B, you can see that if any one of those three circles (detectors) closes its internal contact, it completes the path between the top and bottom wires.

The "General Trade Knowledge" Aspect: Supervision

While Diagram B shows the parallel connection, a real-world fire circuit also includes an End-of-Line (EOL) Resistor.

  1. The resistor allows a tiny "trickle" of current to flow constantly.
  2. If a wire breaks, the trickle stops, and the panel shows a Trouble signal.
  3. If a detector closes (Diagram B), the current bypasses the resistor, causing a huge spike in flow, which the panel interprets as an Alarm.
