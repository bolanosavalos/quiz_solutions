To disconnect the ammeter safely in this specific 3-phase setup, you need to short the lines leading to the meter at the selector switch or the terminal block, not the high-voltage primary lines.

Looking at your image, the ammeter is connected through a Phase Selector Switch (the blue rotary dial). This switch is designed to route current from CT-1, CT-2, or CT-3 to the single ammeter.

Which Lines to Short?

In this wiring configuration, you should short the secondary wires at the yellow terminal block (labeled L1, L2, L3, C) or use the shorting feature of the selector switch if it has one.

  • L1 (Red CT secondary): Short this to the common (C) to bypass CT-1.
  • L2 (Yellow CT secondary): Short this to the common (C) to bypass CT-2.
  • L3 (Blue CT secondary): Short this to the common (C) to bypass CT-3.
  • C (Common/Neutral): This is the return path for all three CTs. 

How to do it safely

  1. Identify the Shorting Bridge: Many of these yellow terminal blocks have a built-in metal bridge or "shorting pin."
  2. Engage the Short: Before you touch the wires on the back of the Ammeter, you must place a jumper across the CT secondary terminals (e.g., L1 to C).
  3. Verify: Once the jumper is secure, the ammeter should read zero even if the load is running. This confirms the current is safely bypassing the meter.
  4. Disconnect: You can now safely remove the wires from the ammeter terminals.

Critical Warning

Do not disconnect any secondary wires while the system is under load without a shorting jumper in place. If you accidentally "open" the loop between a CT and the common line, the voltage will spike instantly to dangerous levels, which can cause an electrical arc or destroy the CT insulation.

You are absolutely right: shorting the ammeter is for when you want to replace the meter while keeping the system running.

However, if your goal is to remove the CT itself (the doughnut-shaped sensors around the wires), you have a much bigger job on your hands. Here is how you handle that safely:

1. Power MUST be turned off

You cannot safely remove a CT while the primary wires are energized.

  • Safety first: You must switch off the MCCB (the main breaker at the top) and follow proper Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures.
  • Physical Removal: Since the primary power cables pass through the center of the CTs, you usually have to disconnect the large power cables from the breaker or the load to slide the CT off the wire.

2. Why you still short them first (The "Best Practice")

Even though you are turning off the main power, seasoned electricians will often short the secondary terminals (L1, L2, L3 to C) before they start turning off the main breaker.

Why?

  • It ensures that if someone accidentally turns the power back on while you are working on the control wires, the CT won't develop a lethal open-circuit voltage.
  • It protects the CT internal windings from any inductive kicks during the power-down sequence.

The Removal Process

If you are removing the CTs permanently or for replacement, follow this order:

  1. Short the Secondaries: Place jumpers across the yellow terminal blocks (L1-C, L2-C, L3-C).
  2. Kill the Main Power: Open the MCCB and verify zero voltage on the primary lines.
  3. Disconnect Secondary Wiring: Remove the thin wires going from the CTs to the terminal block.
  4. Disconnect Primary Cables: Unbolt the thick red, yellow, and blue power cables from the lugs.
  5. Slide CTs Off: Remove the CTs from the cables.
