The correct answer is: changing the primary to a lower voltage tap.

Why this works

To understand why we move to a lower tap to increase the output, we have to look at the transformer turns ratio formula:

Vs=Vp×(NsNp)V_s = V_p \times \left( \frac{N_s}{N_p} \right)

Where:

  • VsV_s = Secondary voltage (Output)
  • VpV_p = Primary voltage (Input)
  • NsN_s = Number of turns in the secondary winding
  • NpN_p = Number of turns in the primary winding

The Logic

Since the secondary turns (NsN_s) are fixed, the only way to increase the output voltage (VsV_s) without changing the input line voltage is to decrease the number of primary turns (NpN_p).

  1. Inverse Relationship: The output voltage is inversely proportional to the number of primary turns.
  2. Higher Ratio: By selecting a lower voltage tap on the primary side, you are effectively reducing the number of turns the input voltage flows through.
  3. Result: This increases the "turns ratio" in favor of the secondary, which boosts the output voltage back up to the desired level.

Why the other options aren't ideal:

  • Raising the line voltage: Usually not possible, as line voltage is determined by the utility or a main power source.
  • Changing to a higher voltage tap: This would increase NpN_p, which would actually drop your output voltage even further.
