The power factor (PF) is defined as the ratio of real power (or working power) to apparent power flowing in an AC circuit. âš¡

In mathematical terms, the formula is:

PF=Real Power (kW)Apparent Power (kVA)\text{PF} = \frac{\text{Real Power (kW)}}{\text{Apparent Power (kVA)}}

  • Real Power (PP) (measured in kilowatts, kW) is the power actually consumed by the load to perform useful work (e.g., generating heat, light, or motion).
  • Apparent Power (SS) (measured in kilovolt-amperes, kVA) is the total power supplied to the circuit, which is the vector sum of real power and reactive power. It is the product of the RMS voltage and current.

Understanding the Power Factor

The power factor is a measure of electrical efficiency, indicating how effectively electrical power is being converted into useful work.

  • It is a unitless number that ranges from 0 to 1.0 (or 0% to 100%).
  • A power factor close to 1.0 (or unity) means the system is highly efficient, as most of the apparent power is being used as real power to do work.
  • A low power factor indicates poor efficiency, where a larger portion of the apparent power is reactive power, which does no useful work but still flows through the system, taking up capacity and causing losses.
