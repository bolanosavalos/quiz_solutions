CEC Rule 12-1608 for Surface Raceways (both metal and non-metallic) specifies the following maximum voltage:

The voltage between conductors contained in surface raceways shall not exceed 300 V\mathbf{300 \text{ V}}, unless the raceways are marked for a higher voltage.

Why the 300 V\mathbf{300 \text{ V}} Limit is the Key Answer

This Rule is a common electrical exam question because it represents a restriction on general-use wiring methods:

  1. General Voltage: Most branch circuits in Canada use 120/240 V120/240 \text{ V} or 120/208 V120/208 \text{ V} (which are well below 300 V300 \text{ V} between conductors). However, industrial or commercial systems may use 600 V600 \text{ V} or 347/600 V347/600 \text{ V} systems, which exceed the 300 V300 \text{ V} limit.
  2. Physical Protection: Surface raceways, especially the smaller and thinner types commonly used for running wires along a wall, do not offer the same level of mechanical protection as a full-size rigid or EMT conduit. The 300 V300 \text{ V} limit is a safeguard.
  3. The Exception: The key phrase in the rule is "unless the raceways are marked for a higher voltage." This allows manufacturers to design and certify heavy-duty surface raceways that meet specific standards (like a wall thickness of 1.02 mm1.02 \text{ mm} for metal raceway in older codes) to permit 600 V600 \text{ V} systems.
  4. Default Value: If the raceway is a generic, unmarked surface type, the default maximum system voltage you can install in it is 300 V300 \text{ V} between conductors.

Therefore, 300 V300 \text{ V} is the correct answer to any question asking for the standard maximum voltage permitted in a surface raceway according to Rule 12-1608.
