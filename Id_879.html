The current flow in the neutral conductor would be 15A15A.

This is determined by applying Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) to the point where the loads connect to the neutral, or simply by finding the difference between the currents in the two ungrounded (hot) conductors.

Explanation

In a single-phase, three-wire 120/240120/240-volt system (common in residential applications):

  1. The two ungrounded (hot) conductors are 180∘180^\circ out of phase with each other.
  2. The neutral conductor carries the unbalanced current between the two hot legs.

The currents are:

  • Current in Hot Leg 1 (I1I_1) =30A= 30A
  • Current in Hot Leg 2 (I2I_2) =15A= 15A (This is the current returning through the neutral line in the diagram)

The current in the neutral conductor (INI_N) is the vector difference between the two hot leg currents, because they are 180∘180^\circ out of phase:

IN=∣I1−I2∣I_N = |I_1 - I_2|

IN=∣30A−15A∣I_N = |30A - 15A|

IN=15AI_N = 15A

  • If the loads were perfectly balanced (30A30A on both hot legs), the neutral current would be 0A0A.
  • Since the load is unbalanced, the neutral carries the difference, which is 15A15A.
